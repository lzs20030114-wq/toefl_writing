L
Dr. Lin教授We've been examining how schools assess student learning. A handful of districts have begun eliminating letter grades in the early years, replacing them with detailed written feedback on specific skills. Advocates argue this reduces anxiety and keeps students focused on growth rather than competition, while skeptics worry it leaves students unprepared for the ranked, high-stakes environments they'll eventually face. Should elementary schools replace traditional grades with narrative feedback?
P
Phoebe同学I think narrative feedback is the better approach for young children. At that age, a letter grade tells a student almost nothing actionable, whereas a teacher describing precisely what to improve actually advances learning. Competition can be introduced gradually once foundational skills are solid.
J
Jared同学I see the appeal, but I worry about the transition. Students raised entirely without grades may struggle when suddenly confronted with rankings in secondary school. Some early exposure to evaluation, handled gently, might build resilience rather than undermine it.
