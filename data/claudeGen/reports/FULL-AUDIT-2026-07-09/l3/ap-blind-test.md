## 第 1 题　(art_history / Gothic architecture)

The flying buttress is an arched stone support that braces a wall from the outside, transferring the outward thrust of a vaulted roof down to a freestanding pier. This invention, perfected by Gothic builders in twelfth-century France, solved a problem that had long constrained cathedral design. [■] Earlier Romanesque churches relied on thick, solid walls to bear the heavy roof, which left little room for windows and produced dim, fortress-like interiors. By moving the structural load outside the building, the flying buttress allowed walls to become thinner and taller. [■] Masons could now pierce them with vast expanses of glass without fear of collapse. [■] The downward pressure of the vault was caught by the arch, carried across an open span, and grounded in the external pier. [■] This redistribution of force transformed the experience of sacred space. Walls dissolved into towering windows of stained glass, flooding the interior with colored light that medieval worshippers read as a vision of the heavens. The technique enabled the soaring heights of cathedrals such as Chartres and Amiens. However, the slender designs it permitted sometimes proved unstable, and several ambitious vaults collapsed before builders learned the limits of stone.

## 第 2 题　(environmental_science / climate change)

Climate sensitivity is the standard term that researchers use to describe how strongly Earth's average surface temperature responds to a doubling of atmospheric carbon dioxide. The figure is usually expressed in degrees Celsius and serves as a single, convenient number for comparing different climate models. Because the temperature change does not happen immediately, climate sensitivity refers to the eventual, fully adjusted warming once the oceans and land surfaces have caught up.

The sensitivity figure depends on a chain of feedback processes. [■] As surfaces warm, ice and snow melt, exposing darker rock and water that absorb still more solar energy. [■] Warmer air also holds more water vapor, itself a powerful greenhouse gas, while shifts in cloud cover can either amplify or moderate the initial heating. [■] Climate scientists estimate each of these contributions separately and then combine them, which is why published sensitivity values are not a single number but a range.

Understanding sensitivity matters for policy. A higher value implies that any given emissions trajectory will produce more severe warming, leaving less room for delay. [■] Studies of past warm periods preserved in deep-sea sediments and ice cores have helped narrow the plausible range, and recent assessments place sensitivity between roughly 2.5 and 4 degrees Celsius. These bounds shape forecasts that governments now use to set emissions targets and infrastructure investments for coming decades.

## 第 3 题　(physical sciences / superconductivity)

Superconductivity is a state in which certain materials conduct electricity with absolutely no resistance once cooled below a specific critical temperature. In an ordinary conductor such as copper, electrons collide with vibrating atoms, scattering and losing energy as heat. A superconductor eliminates this loss entirely, so an electric current set flowing in a closed loop can circulate for years without diminishing. This property emerges abruptly, not gradually, the moment the material crosses its critical temperature.

The underlying explanation involves electrons pairing up rather than traveling alone. [■] Below the critical temperature, subtle vibrations in the atomic lattice draw electrons together into linked couples known as Cooper pairs. [■] These pairs move through the material in a coordinated way, gliding past atoms instead of colliding with them. [■] Because the pairs share a single quantum state, no individual collision can disrupt the flow, and resistance vanishes. [■]

This frictionless conduction promises enormous practical value. Superconducting wires can carry immense currents in compact spaces, powering the magnets inside medical scanners and experimental fusion reactors. Engineers envision power grids that waste none of the electricity they transmit and trains that float above magnetic tracks. However, most known superconductors demand extreme cooling near absolute zero, an expensive requirement that severely restricts where the technology can realistically be deployed.

## 第 4 题　(materials_science / alloys)

Alloys are metallic materials made by combining a base metal with one or more other elements to improve properties such as strength, hardness, or resistance to corrosion. The added elements disrupt the orderly arrangement of atoms in the base metal, making it harder for the metal's internal layers to slide past one another. This atomic interference, known as solid-solution strengthening, is the foundation of nearly all engineering metals in use today.

This strengthening mechanism becomes especially visible in steel, an alloy of iron and a small amount of carbon. [■] The carbon atoms lodge in the gaps between iron atoms and obstruct the movement of defects that would otherwise let the metal deform easily. [■] By carefully adjusting the carbon content, metallurgists can produce steels that range from soft and bendable to extremely hard. [■]

Beyond steel, the same principle guides the design of high-performance alloys for demanding settings. Aluminum alloyed with copper, for instance, becomes light yet strong enough for aircraft frames. [■] Despite their versatility, these materials face limits: adding too much of an element can make an alloy brittle, so each composition reflects a careful balance that engineers may struggle to optimize.

## 第 5 题　(technology / steam power)

A steam engine is a machine that converts the thermal energy of pressurized steam into mechanical motion. Early versions, built to pump water from deep mines, burned huge amounts of coal for little work. The chief fault lay in the single cylinder, which was heated by incoming steam and then cooled by water on every stroke. [■] Each cycle thus wasted most of its fuel reheating metal that the previous stroke had chilled. This heating and cooling was the obstacle the engineer James Watt set out to remove. In the 1760s he added a separate condenser, a chamber where steam could be cooled without cooling the main cylinder. [■] The cylinder could now stay hot from stroke to stroke, so far less coal was needed to raise fresh steam. [■] Watt later linked the piston to a rotating wheel, letting the engine turn machinery rather than merely pump. This rotary motion transformed the engine from a mine pump into the driving force of mills and, eventually, locomotives. [■] Workshops that had depended on flowing rivers could now be built wherever coal was available, freeing manufacturers from fixed water sources. However, the machines stayed heavy and burned fuel steadily, so they suited large enterprises far better than small workshops that could not justify the expense.
